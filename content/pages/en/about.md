# About CardNav

CardNav started from a very practical problem. At first, I simply wanted to use AI more smoothly. When I tried to use overseas models such as GPT, Claude, and Gemini in a stable way, I quickly found that the hard part was not knowing that these models existed. The hard part was the whole path from learning and choosing to actually getting started.

You may need to decide which model to use, which access method makes sense, and how to handle accounts, merchants, network environment, phone numbers, payments, regional limits, and risk controls. These issues rarely appear one by one. They often connect with each other and turn something that should be straightforward into something fragmented, complex, and time-consuming.

## Why This Site Exists

I am a developer from mainland China. CardNav did not begin with a grand idea. It grew slowly after I ran into the same problems again and again. I often had many pages open at the same time, comparing products, prices, stock, and descriptions from different merchants, trying to understand which products were actually the same service, which pages were no longer useful, and which low prices came with higher risk.

The more I searched, the more I felt this should not be so exhausting. The original motivation was simple: I wanted a more convenient entry point for myself, one that organized scattered information and reduced the cost of browsing everywhere, asking around, and learning by trial and error.

For many users, the real question is not just "where can I buy an account." The real questions are:

- Should I choose an official subscription, third-party top-up, ready-made account, API relay, or another method?
- Which merchants are worth checking first, and which products are just different names for the same thing?
- Which region has a better price, and is the payment path actually workable?
- How should I judge network environment, IP, phone verification, and KYC risk in advance?
- Which information is still valid, and which links or tips are already outdated?

These questions have long been scattered across chats, forums, short tutorials, and merchant pages. Many people repeatedly search for the same answers, hit the same traps, and make the same judgments. I gradually realized that this was not only my own inconvenience. It was a long-running information organization problem. CardNav is not essentially about selling things. It is about making this path clearer, concentrating useful information, and making judgment easier.

## More Than Merchant Aggregation

CardNav did begin with merchant and product information. That was the most direct layer of value: gather scattered AI shops, merchants, stock, prices, and purchase entry points into one place so users can browse, filter, and compare faster.

As the site has evolved, it has become more than an AI shop directory. CardNav is gradually connecting more key steps:

- Model leaderboards to help users understand which models fit their tasks
- Official subscription price comparisons to show regional price differences and access costs
- Structured guide content covering model choice, access paths, merchant screening, network setup, payment methods, and risk control
- Practical tools such as IP purity checks and credential format conversion
- Merchant submission, listing, and partnership entry points so the supply side also has a clearer public path

In other words, CardNav is moving from "help you find merchants" toward "help you use AI more smoothly" across a fuller journey.

## Who It Serves

CardNav first serves people who genuinely need AI but do not want to be repeatedly drained by fragmented information and high trial-and-error costs. At the beginning, I understood the problem mainly from the perspective of Chinese users. Later, I learned that this situation is not limited to China. Users in places such as Russia, Iran, and other countries or regions can also face clear barriers around access, registration, payment, and daily use.

That makes CardNav more than an information tool for Chinese users. It has room to grow into broader international scenarios and serve more people facing similar difficulties.

CardNav also serves merchants. Merchants are not only data sources being listed; they are active participants in this ecosystem. A clearer public entry point helps users find information faster and helps merchants gain more stable exposure, listing paths, and partnership opportunities.

## Where It Is Going

CardNav will not stop at being only an AI shop directory. Merchant aggregation is an entry point, not the destination.

I hope it keeps growing into a one-stop AI access platform. When users want to use a model, they should not have to search everywhere, ask the same questions repeatedly, or learn everything through failed attempts. They should be able to solve most of the path here: what to choose, how to start, and how to reduce risk and improve success rate.

That is why CardNav will keep improving in these directions:

- Connect models, prices, merchants, tools, and knowledge content into a more natural path
- Fill in key questions around network access, payment, risk control, regional differences, and practical tools
- Reduce information gaps, trial-and-error costs, and repeated work during AI use
- Make the site solve not only "where to buy" but also "how to use AI more smoothly"

If the earlier CardNav was an entry point for discovering merchants, the future CardNav aims to become a genuinely useful one-stop AI entry point: helping users take fewer detours and helping merchants be seen more efficiently.
