# 4. Risk control and daily pitfall avoidance during AI subscription use

> This article is provided by **CardNav [cardnav.xyz](https://cardnav.xyz)**.

Registering an account and binding a payment card are often only the first step. During daily use of overseas large model web services or services, platforms usually continue to perform risk-control review. If certain behaviors are identified by the system as abnormal, even normally paid subscription accounts may face account bans, forced verification, or payment restrictions.

Mainstream large model platforms usually combine IP, browser fingerprint, payment information, and behavior patterns for risk identification.

## Frequent IP and network environment drift
<!-- badge="IP risk" icon="vpn" -->

Frequent switching of login regions and multi-person shared proxies will make the account environment look unstable. The response strategy is fixed region, fixed device, and fixed route.

- Switch across regions less
- Try not to use public proxies
- Do not log in to the same account everywhere

## Linked IP risk control and free account abuse traps
<!-- badge="Linked risk" icon="shield-alert" -->

If multiple accounts share the same IP or the same batch of environments for a long time, as long as one account has a problem, other accounts may also be pulled into review. The response strategy is to use one environment per account as much as possible.

- Running multiple accounts in parallel amplifies risk
- Free trials are usually stricter
- Low-price ready-made accounts have higher uncertainty

## Reverse proxies and relay tools not updated in time
<!-- badge="Toolchain risk" icon="toolbox" -->

If reverse proxies, relay sites, or open-source clients are not updated for a long time, the risk will rise significantly. The response strategy is to update in time and not keep old versions running for a long time.

- Security gateways keep upgrading
- Old versions are more likely to expose signatures
- The older the tool, the higher the risk

## Compliance red lines in conversation content
<!-- badge="Content risk" icon="shield-alert" -->

If you continuously induce generation of content that clearly violates compliance policies, it usually increases the probability of the account being restricted, reviewed, or frozen. The response strategy is not to use the main account to repeatedly test sensitive boundaries.

- Continuously challenging rules is more dangerous
- Sensitive content is more likely to alert
- The more alerts there are, the harder it is to predict

## Payment information and usage region mismatch
<!-- badge="Payment risk" icon="cash" -->

When payment card region, billing address, and login region are obviously mismatched, payment-side review is more easily triggered. The response strategy is to keep payment information and common environment as consistent as possible.

- Keep billing information as consistent as possible
- Do not rush to pay across regions with a new account
- Mix high-risk payment methods less

## Behavior pattern looks like bulk abuse or automation
<!-- badge="Usage pattern" icon="route" -->

High-frequency concurrent use by new accounts in a short time, multiple accounts sharing the same environment, and repeated rule challenges all make behavior look unlike normal personal use. The response strategy is to slow down the pace and use it like a real person.

- Use new accounts at low frequency first
- Share environments across multiple accounts less
- Do not repeatedly probe boundaries

## Abnormal browser environment or access method
<!-- badge="Highly sensitive signal" icon="shield-alert" -->

Automation scripts, headless browsers, reverse-engineered web APIs, and request-enhancing plugins more easily leave abnormal signatures. The response strategy is to use official entries as much as possible and change the browser environment less.

- Install fewer plugins that rewrite requests
- Do not use scripts to control the web end
- Touch unofficial bridge tools less

## Fixed system prompts from third-party clients
<!-- badge="Prompt risk" icon="model" -->

Many unofficial clients, Agents, or enhancement tools mount fixed system prompts or request templates for a long time. The response strategy is to use fewer third-party clients with overly strong signatures.

- Fixed templates are more easily clustered and identified
- They look more like third-party bridging or bulk calling

## If the account is banned, how to handle it

## Start an appeal as soon as possible
<!-- badge="First step" icon="support" -->

If you confirm that you used it normally, first use the official appeal process and explain the account usage situation.

- Submit the appeal immediately
- Explain account usage
- Keep the ban notice and order records

## Apply for a refund if recovery fails
<!-- badge="Second step" icon="cash" -->

If the appeal is not approved, or the account can no longer continue to be used, handling the refund afterward is usually more stable.

- Prepare deduction records and order numbers
- Explain that the account can no longer continue to be used
- Handle refunds and appeals separately

## Handle App Store / Google Play separately
<!-- badge="App store" icon="cart" -->

If your subscription was opened through App Store or Google Play, you can also apply for a refund through the app store channel.

- Find Apple for App Store orders
- Find Google for Google Play orders
- Such orders are usually handled according to store rules
